01 Executive Summary

1.1 Product Overview

ORVIKA AI is a Windows desktop application that allows individuals and organizations to analyze sensitive documents — PDFs, research papers, business reports, meeting recordings, and videos — using AI, without ever uploading the original files to the cloud.

The application processes all private data locally on the user's device using a local AI model (Gemma 4). When deeper research is needed, the local agent formulates abstract research queries — scrubbed of any user data — and sends only those queries to cloud-based AI services (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic). The cloud returns public research, which the local model combines with the private analysis to produce a comprehensive final answer.

1.2 Core Innovation — Hybrid Privacy Architecture

The fundamental innovation is the separation of private reasoning from public research. Instead of sending user documents to the cloud for analysis, the system:

  1. Analyzes privately — The local model reads, understands, and reasons over user files entirely on-device.
  2. Abstracts research needs — The local agent converts knowledge gaps into generic research topics stripped of all user data.
  3. Researches publicly — The cloud agent researches only public information based on abstract topics.
  4. Synthesizes locally — The local model merges private analysis with public research into the final output.

1.3 Why Now

  1. Privacy regulations are tightening worldwide — GDPR, CCPA, China's PIPL, EU AI Act, and sector-specific mandates (HIPAA, SOX, ITAR, FedRAMP) are creating hard constraints on how data can be processed.
  2. Enterprise AI adoption is gated by security concerns — A 2025 McKinsey survey found that 65% of enterprises cite data privacy as the #1 barrier to AI adoption.
  3. Local AI models are becoming viable on consumer hardware — Gemma 4, Llama, and Phi models now deliver meaningful reasoning quality on 8–16 GB RAM machines.
  4. Trust in cloud AI providers is eroding — High-profile data incidents and opaque data handling policies have made users and organizations wary.
  5. No existing product solves the hybrid problem — Cloud AI is powerful but leaks data; local AI is private but weak. No product bridges the gap with transparency.

1.4 Market Opportunity

The global AI market is projected to exceed $500B by 2027. The privacy-preserving AI segment — defined as products that process sensitive data without cloud exfiltration — is estimated at $8–12B and growing at 35% CAGR as regulated industries adopt AI under compliance constraints.

02 Problem Statement

2.1 The Privacy Problem with Cloud AI

When users upload files to cloud-based AI assistants (ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude, etc.), their data leaves the device and enters third-party infrastructure. This creates five categories of serious concern:

ConcernDescription
Data PrivacySensitive personal or business information is transmitted to and potentially stored on third-party servers
Confidential Business InformationFinancial reports, strategic plans, and internal memos could be exposed
Intellectual PropertyTrade secrets, patents-in-progress, and proprietary research risk leakage
Regulatory ComplianceHealthcare (HIPAA), finance (SOX, GLBA), government (ITAR, FedRAMP) restrict data handling
User TrustUsers are increasingly uncomfortable with data collection practices of cloud AI providers

2.2 Compliance Barriers to Enterprise AI Adoption

Regulated industries face hard legal constraints — not preferences — on data handling:

IndustryRegulationKey Constraint
HealthcareHIPAAProtected health information (PHI) cannot leave controlled environments
FinanceSOX, GLBANon-public financial data must remain within audited infrastructure
LegalAttorney-Client PrivilegePrivileged communications cannot be processed by third parties without consent
GovernmentITAR, FedRAMP, CMMCClassified or controlled information must remain on authorized systems
ResearchUniversity IRB, Export ControlsUnpublished findings and controlled research data have handling restrictions

These are not soft preferences. Violating them can result in criminal liability, loss of professional licenses, contract termination, and regulatory sanctions.

2.3 Enterprise Adoption Challenges

  • Shadow AI risk — Employees use personal ChatGPT accounts for work documents because no compliant alternative exists.
  • Vendor lock-in — Cloud AI providers change terms, pricing, and data handling policies unilaterally.
  • Audit requirements — Regulated industries require audit trails for every data processing event.
  • Data residency — Some jurisdictions require data to remain within geographic boundaries.

2.4 Local AI Limitations

Fully local AI solutions (Ollama, LM Studio, LocalAI) solve the privacy problem but create capability problems:

  • Requires powerful hardware — 16 GB+ RAM, dedicated GPU for acceptable performance.
  • Smaller, weaker models — Local models have lower reasoning quality than frontier cloud models.
  • No current information — Local models have no access to real-time data, recent publications, or market information.
  • Complex setup — Non-technical users struggle with model download, quantization, and configuration.

2.5 The Market Gap

The Gap No existing product combines local-first privacy for sensitive data with cloud-powered research depth for public information — while giving users full transparency into what leaves their device. This gap exists at the intersection of three converging trends: rising AI capability, tightening privacy regulation, and growing enterprise AI demand.

03 Vision

3.1 Long-Term Vision

Create a privacy-first AI operating environment where files remain local, AI agents collaborate intelligently, cloud models provide research-only capabilities, and users have complete visibility into what leaves their device. Within 2–3 years, this evolves into a platform where AI understands your local files contextually, assists across applications without data leaving the device, and enables federated research sharing without source document exposure.

3.2 Mission

Democratize access to advanced AI research capabilities for every individual and organization — regardless of their data sensitivity constraints.

20 CTO Review

20.1 Architectural Strengths

  1. Clean separation of concerns — Local processing, cloud research, and security are well-isolated layers with clear interfaces.
  2. Tauri is the right choice — Smaller bundle, Rust backend for security-critical code, native performance, allowlist permissions.
  3. Offline-first architecture — Cloud is an enhancement, not a requirement.
  4. FAISS for vector search — Mature, high-performance, well-understood.
  5. llama.cpp for inference — Active community, broad hardware support, efficient quantization.

20.2 Architectural Weaknesses

20.5 Top 10 Recommendations Before Development Starts

  1. Validate Gemma 4 on minimum hardware (8 GB RAM, no GPU) before committing. If quality or speed is unacceptable, adjust model strategy. Do this in Week 1.
  2. Build the sanitization pipeline first and test it adversarially. Start with 1,000 synthetic test cases, grow to 10,000+ before beta. If sanitization can't be made reliable, the product premise fails.
  3. Implement network traffic monitoring as a development tool. Every developer should see exactly what the application sends over the wire. This catches bugs early and builds confidence.
  4. Design the inference abstraction layer from day one. Don't hardcode llama.cpp calls. Create a LocalLLM trait in Rust with a clean interface. Implement llama.cpp as one backend.
  5. Start with PDF only and nail the experience. Get parsing, chunking, embedding, retrieval, and Q&A working perfectly before adding other formats.
  6. Establish a benchmark suite before writing feature code. Define baselines (TTFT, generation speed, parse time, search latency) and run on every commit. Regressions block merges.
  7. Choose a prompt engineering framework and stick with it. Prompt templates should be version-controlled, testable, and separate from application code.
  8. Plan for Windows-specific edge cases from the start. File paths (backslashes, unicode, long paths), Windows Defender false positives, UAC, Credential Manager integration.
  9. Set up a privacy-focused CI pipeline. In addition to standard CI, add sanitization test suite and network monitoring on every PR. Regressions block merges.
  10. Hire or designate a security champion from Day 1. This person owns the threat model, reviews every outbound path, maintains adversarial tests, and has veto power over privacy-affecting changes. Non-negotiable.
